\section{Towards Weakly Supervised Temporal Uncertainty Localization}
\label{sec:non_static}

Mechanistic detectors face a tension between coverage and scalability.
Static probing classifiers \citep{azaria2023,orgad2024} operate on a
single layer--token position and ignore signal elsewhere in $A$; the
optimal position varies across samples, datasets, and LLMs, often
requiring an external search \citep{orgad2024}. Methods that process
the whole tensor capture distributed signal but are ill-suited to
real-time long-form generation: some pool the token axis into a fixed
size, diluting signal as responses grow; others remain accurate at
long context but require one forward per token. Even when a
full-tensor method does scale, a single sequence-level score does not
say \emph{where} the suspicious activations lie -- a poor fit for
responses where most tokens are correct and only a few are
problematic.

\paragraph{Signals are distributed and LLM-specific.} Following
\citet{orgad2024} on Mis-7B \citep{albertqjiang2023}, we train per
layer--token logistic regression classifiers and visualize ROC-AUC
heatmaps. Tokens are restricted to
$\mathcal{N} = \{0, 1, 2, -3, -2, -1\}$ (first/last three positions).
Figure~\ref{fig:heatmaps} shows the heatmap on Movies
\citep{orgad2024} for three LLMs; the appendix carries the full set.
Predictive signals spread across many $(L, T)$ cells, but the most
informative positions shift across LLMs: middle layers for Mis-7B and
LlaMa-8B, late layers for Qwen-7B. The best position for Mis-7B,
$(14, 0)$, transfers to LlaMa-8B and Qwen-7B with $\approx 7\%$ AUC
loss -- no fixed slice suffices.

\paragraph{Tensor methods scale poorly with response length.}
Figure~\ref{fig:perf_by_length} reports per-token AUROC on LongFact as
prefix length $T$ grows. ACT-ViT with its default short pooled token
axis peaks at very short prefixes but collapses past $T = 48$,
dropping below chance for $T \in \{192, 384\}$ as more tokens are
squeezed through a fixed-size pooled image. Widening the pooled axis
from 6 to 186 patches the long-prefix collapse but does not recover
the short-prefix peak. Figure~\ref{fig:inference_time} reports the
matching wall-clock cost on one $T = 384$ passage: ACT-ViT needs $T$
sequential forwards, so total latency scales linearly with response
length. A per-token model that produces all logits in a single
forward is $\approx 25\times$ faster while keeping a flat AUROC
profile across the prefix grid.

\paragraph{From sequence-level detection to localization.} For
long-form generation a single score is not actionable. We aim to
additionally produce per-window scores
$\{\hat{p}^{(t)}\}_{t=1}^{T}$ that localize the generated-token
regions whose activations most contribute to the prediction.

\paragraph{The weak-supervision challenge.} Hallucination labels are
typically available only at the response level. Extracting atomic
claims and broadcasting their verification result to every token of
the span yields noisy token labels: spans contain non-hallucinated
tokens, and the relevant activation signal may appear \emph{before}
the false claim is even generated.
Figure~\ref{fig:signal_propagation} measures this directly: on
RAGTruth, a linear probe's ROC-AUC ramps from chance at offset $-24$
to $0.66$ at the token immediately before a hallucinated span, rises
monotonically through the span ($0.71 \to 0.79$), and decays slowly
afterward. We therefore treat span labels as coarse auxiliary
supervision and rely on weak supervision to localize within.

\paragraph{Weakly supervised temporal localization.} ACT-ViT shows
that the $(L,T)$ plane has spatial structure amenable to visual
encoders, but a single fixed-size image fits poorly when $T$ ranges
from a handful to thousands of tokens. We frame $A$ as a \emph{video}
over the token axis and borrow WSTL from video understanding
\citep{pilhyeonlee2021}. Each token is a frame whose height is the
layer axis and whose channels are the hidden dimension; a
\emph{window} aggregates $W$ consecutive tokens. The neighbourhood
structure of Figure~\ref{fig:signal_propagation} is exactly what a
window exploits: surrounding tokens emit usable signal about whether
a target token is hallucinated, so the model can localize spans of
uncertainty without window-level labels.

\paragraph{Three-stage training.} Learning sequence-level uncertainty
and window-level localization jointly under weak supervision is a
hard optimization. We decompose training into three stages.
\textbf{Stage 1} trains the encoder on full activation tensors with
sequence-level labels. \textbf{Stage 2} freezes the encoder and trains a Mamba-2 temporal
head over per-window encodings via top-$k$ MIL against the same
sequence label, so the head learns to localize the uncertainty signal
to a few windows inside each span without per-window supervision; we
use Mamba \citep{albertgu2024} for its linear-time long-sequence
inference.
\textbf{Stage 3} trains the head across spans in the full sequence
rather than on isolated atom segments: fine-tuning on whole passages
with per-atom auxiliary labels teaches it how the uncertainty
activation signal changes across claim spans, in particular on
mixed-content windows whose receptive field contains both hallucinated
and supported tokens (which Stages~1--2 never see). We describe each stage in the next section.
