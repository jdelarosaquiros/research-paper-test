\begin{abstract}
Detecting hallucinations in Large Language Model-generated text is crucial for
their safe deployment. Recent mechanistic approaches show promise by using
internal hidden states as uncertainty signals, but they face two important
limitations. Some methods probe only isolated layer--token positions, missing
uncertainty signals distributed across layers and generated tokens. Others
process richer activation tensors, but are designed mainly for sequence-level
prediction and do not localize where uncertainty arises, or are too costly for
real-time long-form generation. In this paper, we introduce a temporal
uncertainty localization framework that predicts hallucination likelihood while
identifying the generated-token regions whose hidden-state activations most
contribute to uncertainty. We build on the structured nature of activation
tensors, treating the layer--token hidden-state plane as an activation image,
and extend this view with a temporal localization head over token windows.
Since hallucination labels are typically available only at the full-response
level, we train our model using weak supervision: a frozen ACT-ViT encoder
extracts uncertainty-aware features from local activation windows, and a
Mamba-based temporal head assigns uncertainty logits to each window. A top-$k$
multiple-instance learning objective aggregates the most uncertain windows into
a sequence-level prediction, enabling localization without segment-level
labels. Our approach separates representation learning from weak localization:
first learning activation patterns predictive of uncertainty at the
sequence level, then freezing the encoder and training the temporal head to
localize suspicious regions efficiently. This yields a scalable framework for
real-time activation-based uncertainty detection in long-form LLM generation.
\end{abstract}
