\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Uncertainty estimation for LLMs}

Uncertainty estimation has been widely used for hallucination
detection in LLMs. Confidence-based methods derive uncertainty from
the predictive distribution of a single generation, using scores such
as sequence probability, token entropy, negative log-likelihood, or
relevance-weighted variants
\citep{malinin2021uncertainty,duan2024shifting,schweighofer2025rethinking}.
Other methods estimate uncertainty from multiple generations by
measuring semantic agreement, self-consistency, or graph-based
similarity among sampled answers
\citep{kuhn2023semantic,lin2024generating,jiang2024,manakul2023,band2024}.
These approaches can be effective, especially when semantic
consistency is available, but they mainly operate on final outputs and
often require repeated sampling, external entailment models, or
LLM-based judging. As a result, they are costly for long-form
generations and do not directly exploit the internal activation
signals produced during generation.

\subsection{Mechanistic hallucination detection}

Recent work shows that LLM hidden states contain signals about
truthfulness and hallucination risk
\citep{marks2023,azaria2023,liu2024,ji2024}. Many mechanistic
detectors train probes on representations from selected layers or
token positions
\citep{kadavath2022,snyder2024,zou2023,li2024}. These methods are
efficient, but their reliance on fixed layer-token slices can miss
signals whose location changes across models, tasks, and generated
responses. Activation-map methods such as ACT-ViT
\citep{barshalom2025} broaden this view by treating the layer-token
activation plane as a structured input, but they typically pool over
the full response and produce a sequence-level score. This makes them
less suited to long-form outputs, where supported and unsupported
claims can appear in the same generation.

Several methods move toward finer-grained detection. Fact-probe and
related claim-level approaches score extracted claims rather than
entire responses
\citep{snyder2024,he2024}, while token-level methods aim to detect
hallucination signals during generation
\citep{he2024,su2024}. However, claim-level pipelines require claim
extraction or repeated scoring, and isolated token scoring can miss
evidence in the surrounding context. HaMI \citep{niu2025hami} is
closest to our weak-supervision setting: it formulates hallucination
detection as multiple-instance learning over token hidden states and
adaptively selects informative tokens for sequence-level prediction.
Our work shares the sparsity and top-$k$ MIL motivation, but changes
the localization unit from individual token states to local
layer-token activation windows. Our approach also uses temporal information gathered from previous windows. 
This allows TPE to model evidence
that appears before and during an unsupported span, and to
produce token-level localization scores for long-form generations.
