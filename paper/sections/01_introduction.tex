\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) hallucinate -- generate plausible but
unsupported claims -- and detecting these errors is critical in
high-stakes settings.

Many detection methods rely on external tools: judging answers with
another LLM, sampling many responses, or verifying claims via
retrieval. These pipelines are accurate but slow and expensive.
\emph{Mechanistic} methods avoid extra LLM calls and instead inspect
the hidden states of the generating model.

The simplest mechanistic detectors are probes: small classifiers
trained on a fixed layer--token position. Probes are cheap (one
forward pass), but uncertainty signals move across layers, tokens,
tasks, and models, so any fixed slice misses signal elsewhere.
Activation-tensor methods address this by consuming the full hidden
tensor $A \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times T \times D}$ -- $L$ layers, $T$
generated tokens, $D$ hidden dimensions. ACT-ViT treats $A$ as an
image (the $(L,T)$ plane, with $D$ as channels) and captures
distributed signal, but its fixed pooled shape collapses information
as $T$ grows. Other tensor methods avoid this loss only at
prohibitive inference cost.

Even without the scaling cost, a single sequence-level score cannot
say \emph{where} the suspicious regions are, which limits its
usefulness for downstream correction or verification. Localizing
uncertainty within a passage is hard because labels are typically
response-level. The natural workaround -- extract atomic claims,
verify each, and broadcast the verdict to every token of the span --
yields doubly noisy supervision: spans contain non-hallucinated
tokens, and the activation signal predictive of a hallucination often
appears \emph{before} the false claim is generated. Span labels
therefore neither isolate the offending tokens nor cover all the
relevant signal around them.

We frame this as weakly supervised temporal localization (WSTL),
borrowing from video understanding \citep{pilhyeonlee2021}: a video
model learns to localize an action using only a clip-level label,
without per-frame supervision. We apply the same idea to $A$, framed
as a \emph{video} over the token axis -- each token is a frame whose
height is the layer axis and whose channels are the hidden dimension,
and a \emph{window} aggregates $W$ consecutive tokens. The model
learns which windows carry the predictive signal from the
sequence-level label, with no window-level supervision required.

Our method has three stages. \textbf{Stage 1} trains an ACT-ViT
encoder on full activation tensors with sequence-level labels.
\textbf{Stage 2} freezes the encoder, applies it to centered
length-$W$ token windows, and trains a Mamba-based temporal head
\citep{albertgu2024} via top-$k$ multiple-instance learning: the
$k$ most uncertain window logits are averaged into a sequence-level
prediction and supervised against the atom-level label. Stages 1--2
see one atom at a time and never have to separate uncertain atoms
from confident neighbours in a passage. \textbf{Stage 3} closes this
gap: with the encoder frozen, the head is fine-tuned on whole
passages, smoothing the per-token logits along the token axis and
aggregating them within each atom span into a per-atom score
supervised by per-atom binary cross-entropy. Mamba is used for its
linear-time inference, making the method viable for real-time
long-form generation.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We recast mechanistic hallucination detection as weakly supervised
    temporal localization over LLM activation tensors, enabling uncertainty
    scores to be assigned to local regions of a long-form response rather
    than only to the full sequence.

    \item We propose Temp-ViT, a windowed activation-tensor architecture
    that uses an ViT encoder to capture layer--token activation patterns
    and a temporal head to model how uncertainty signals evolve
    across generated tokens.

    \item We introduce a three-stage training curriculum that learns from
    coarse supervision: sequence-level labels for encoder pretraining,
    top-$k$ MIL for per-window localization, and passage-level per-atom
    refinement for ranking uncertain claims against confident neighboring
    claims.

    \item We demonstrate efficient fine-grained detection on long-form
    hallucination benchmarks, evaluating token-, sentence-, and claim-level
    localization, while also showing that the localized scores can be
    aggregated into competitive sequence-level uncertainty estimates on
    LM-Polygraph.
\end{enumerate}
