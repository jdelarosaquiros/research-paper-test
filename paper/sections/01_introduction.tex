\paragraph{Why long-form localization matters.}
Long-form LLM outputs can interleave supported statements with localized factual errors.
A useful detector should therefore identify where unreliable content appears, not only
whether the full response is wrong. This distinction matters for retrieval-augmented
answers, biographies, and long explanations, where a single unsupported span can be
hidden inside otherwise correct text \citep{niu2024ragtruth,min2023,wei2024}.

\paragraph{What current uncertainty methods leave open.}
Predictive uncertainty methods estimate confidence from a single generation through
sequence probability, entropy, or related token-probability statistics
\citep{malinin2021uncertainty,duan2024shifting,schweighofer2025rethinking}.
Multiple-generation methods sample several answers and measure semantic
consistency \citep{kuhn2023semantic,lin2024generating,jiang2024}. Verifier
pipelines decompose the response into claims and check them with external systems
\citep{manakul2023,min2023,zhao2024}. These approaches are useful, but they either
depend on the final generation output, require extra samples or verifiers, or become
slow when applied repeatedly across long passages.
This motivates using internal representations from the generating model.

\paragraph{Mechanistic detectors read the wrong unit.}
Hidden states encode truthfulness and hallucination signals
\citep{azaria2023,marks2023,liu2024,ji2024}, but existing mechanistic detectors
choose a unit that is poorly matched to long-form localization.
Fixed layer-token probes are efficient but too narrow, because the useful signal can
move across layers, positions, models, and tasks \citep{kadavath2022,orgad2024}.
Full-response activation-map probes cover more signal, but pooling a whole response
into one representation produces a sequence-level score and forces a tradeoff between
speed and accuracy as outputs grow longer \citep{barshalom2025}.
Claim-level classifiers localize more coarsely, but they score isolated extracted
claims and can miss pre-span, post-span, and boundary evidence in the surrounding
generation \citep{snyder2024,he2024}.

\paragraph{Our observation.}
Hallucination evidence is temporally local rather than token-isolated.
Figure~\ref{fig:temp_vit_example} illustrates the target behavior: a detector should
assign high uncertainty to the unsupported span while preserving low scores on the
surrounding supported text.
Figure~\ref{fig:signal_propagation} shows why local windows are the right instance
unit: on RAGTruth, a linear probe signal rises before the hallucinated span, increases
through it, and decays after it. The evidence is not concentrated in one selected
token; it forms a local temporal pattern around the hallucinated span.

\paragraph{Temp-ViT.}
We propose Temp-ViT, a weakly supervised temporal localization model for LLM
hidden-state activations. Temp-ViT treats each response as a temporal sequence of
local layer-token activation windows. Each window is encoded as a 2D activation
image over the layer-token plane, and a temporal head propagates window-level
uncertainty evidence across the response \citep{pilhyeonlee2021,albertgu2024}.
The model is trained from weak claim or sequence labels through three stages:
claim-level encoder pretraining, top-$k$ MIL window localization, and cross-claim
refinement over mixed-content windows. Concretely, we contribute:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We formulate mechanistic hallucination detection as weakly supervised
    temporal localization over hidden-state activation windows, enabling per-token predictions
    as granular as token level from weak labels.

    \item We propose Temp-ViT, a windowed activation model that encodes local
    layer-token planes with a ViT encoder and propagates uncertainty evidence with
    a temporal head. We train it with a three-stage weak-supervision framework that learns
    hallucination activation patterns with claim labels, localizes responsible windows with
    top-$k$ MIL, and refines boundary behavior across neighboring correct and
    hallucinated claims.

    \item We demonstrate granular long-form hallucination localization on
    biographical generation, open-ended factual QA, and retrieval-augmented QA
    when using claim-level labels for training. We also show it outperforms other methods
    even when using weaker sequence-level labels.
\end{enumerate}
